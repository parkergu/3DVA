3DVA:Learning Robust 3D Diffusion Policies via Visual Attention

Abstract

Imitation learning offers an effective paradigm for teaching robots, yet acquiring robust and generalizable policies for complex skills often requires a large number of human demonstrations. 3D Diffusion Policy (DP3) learns robust and generalizable skills with greater sample efficiency, requiring fewer demonstrations than prior methods. Although DP3 introduced an efficient architecture, the optimal 3D representation for control remains an open question.

To enhance manipulation capabilities, we introduce 3D Visual Attention Diffusion Policy (3DVA), which enhances DP3 by incorporating a visual attention module. This allows the policy to selectively focus on the most pertinent regions of point cloud observations, leading to more effective representation learning.

In our experiments across 8 simulation tasks, 3DVA surpasses the strong baseline (DP3) with a [X%] relative improvement. Furthermore, on 3 real-world robot tasks, 3DVA demonstrates robust control, achieving an impressive 76% success rate with only 50 demonstrations per task. It also exhibits excellent generalization across variations in object appearance, lighting conditions, and instances. Our extensive evaluations highlight the critical role of visual attention in learning effective 3D representations for robotic manipulation.

Overview

Overview of the proposed system. The proposed system comprises three key components: the data collection system, the 3D visuomotor policy learning method, and the real-world deployment. This integrated system enables the robot to execute autonomous skills in diverse real-world scenes.

CONTROL USING LEARNED DYNAMICS

For manipulation, we use the shooting method for trajectory optimization, and use Model Predictive Control (MPC) to stabilize the trajectory by optimizing actions and executing the first action at every time step as a way to compensate the prediction error.

Real-world Experiments for the test of manipulation speed (inference by GPU)

A Kinova Gen3 robot manipulates one end of a DLO to different desired shapes, and the other end is fixed. In real-world experiments, the DLO is a 0.5-m USB cable and characterized by 8 features (red markers). An Intel Realsense D435 RGBD camera is utilized to obtain the positions of features of the DLO at 15 Hz and 1280 x 720 resolution.
